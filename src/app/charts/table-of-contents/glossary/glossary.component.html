Glossary


Factor Model:

Hundreds of variables or factors affect a company's stock price including inflation, interest rates, company size, leverage, earnings, etc. Each factor impacts individual industries and companies differently. A factor model identifies those variables that have statistically significant power to predict or explain changes in stock prices. Factor models can capture market inefficiencies, use past observations to project the future, and provide quantitative valuations.

DeMarche Factor Model:

The DeMarche Factor Model has identified more than 70 factors that, when combined, have significant explanatory or predictive power. This model was developed by constructing a database covering more than 20 years of monthly information. Using cross-sectional and historical regressions, it tests hundreds of factors of various types.

STYLES:

Value:

DeMarche uses nine factors to rate a stock across the value/growth spectrum. This style of investing is focused heavily on valuation models including low price-to-book, price-to-earnings, price-to-sales and cash flow ratios, often in absolute terms. These managers are also regarded sometimes as "contrarian" as they are often buying stocks against current market sentiment. Value stocks typically have low price-to-earnings ratios while providing higher yields. Generally speaking, these managers may also employ wider sector controls when contrasted to relative value strategies.

Relative Value:

DeMarche uses nine factors to rate a stock across the value/growth spectrum. This style of investing is focused almost entirely on valuation factors including dividend discount modeling, relative price-to-book, price-to-earnings, price-to-sales and cash flow ratios. These factors are generally considered on a relative (versus the market benchmark) basis. Relative value securities often have price-to-earnings ratios and betas that are lower than the market and yields that are greater than the market. Generally speaking, these managers typically will incorporate some element of risk control into their investment process with accompanying benchmark sensitivity.

Relative Growth:

DeMarche uses nine factors to rate a stock across the value/growth spectrum. This style of investing is focused mostly on growth factors including companies exhibiting above-average rates of earnings growth, sales growth and earnings momentum. Relative valuation metrics, including price-to-earnings and price-to-book ratios, are a consideration in this style with the result often referred to as "GARP" or "Growth at a Reasonable Price" as these characteristics are typically above market averages while dividends are below market averages as the companies in the portfolio often seek reinvestment opportunities. Generally speaking, these managers typically will incorporate some element of risk control into their investment process with accompanying benchmark sensitivity.

Growth:

DeMarche uses nine factors to rate a stock across the value/growth spectrum. This style of investing is focused almost entirely on high growth factors including earnings growth, revenue growth, sales growth, improved cash flow, earnings momentum and price-earnings expansion. Valuation is not emphasized as heavily in this style of investing and consequently these strategies are often volatile as evidenced by beta that can be significantly above market. Growth stocks typically feature high price-to-earnings ratios with low, or non-existent yields as companies focus on reinvestment opportunities. Generally speaking, these managers may also employ wider sector controls when contrasted to relative growth strategies.

Aggressive:

DeMarche uses 11 factors to rate a stock as aggressive or defensive. These ratings replace market capitalization as a primary diagnostic tool. Though categorizing a stock as aggressive is not based on cap size, smaller stocks tend to be more aggressive than Large Cap stocks. An aggressive stock may include a small company with high market risk, wide trading ranges, low dividend yields and payout ratios, and thin profit margins.

Defensive:

DeMarche uses 11 factors to rate a stock as aggressive or defensive. These ratings replace market capitalization as a primary diagnostic tool. Defensive stocks tend to be large companies that have low market risk and narrow trading ranges. These stocks also tend to be companies with high dividends and payout ratios. These stocks also may be higher priced than aggressive stocks.

CAP RANGES:

Large Cap:

These managers typically invest only in companies with market caps exceeding $10 billion at purchase. These stocks are typically established companies with household names. For statistical purposes, each Large Cap universe includes some members made up of securities consistent with the investment style using stratified sampling techniques.

Core:

These managers typically invest in companies with market caps that are greater than the S & P 500 median market cap with material representation in Mid Cap companies and very minor exposure to smaller cap companies.

Specialty Large:

These managers typically invest in companies with market caps that are greater than the S & P 500 median market cap with significant representation in Mid and Small Cap companies.

Mid Cap:

These managers typically invest in companies with market caps ranging from $2 billion to $20 billion at purchase. This cap range is dynamic with Mid Cap managers varying significantly. For statistical purposes, each Mid Cap universe includes some members made up of securities consistent with the investment style using stratified sampling techniques.

Specialty Small:

These managers typically invest in Small Cap companies, with some limited representation in larger cap companies. Managers that invest in this space often "let winners run" from Small Cap as the market cap range may not necessarily require the sale of a stock for an extended period of time.

Small Cap:

These managers typically invest in companies with market caps less than $2 billion. Liquidity can be a concern when investing in Small Cap stocks.

Micro Cap:

These managers typically invest in emerging companies with market caps less than $1 billion at purchase. In many cases, the manager may target investments in companies that have a market cap of less than $100 million. Trading liquidity is often an area of emphasis when investing in Micro Cap stocks.

RISK ANALYSIS DEFINITIONS:

Alpha:

A measure of the manager's excess return, i.e. contribution to performance. It is the portion of a portfolio return that is not explained by systematic risk. Alpha is calculated by regressing the portfolio's excess return on a relevant benchmark's excess return.

Beta:

Beta measures the sensitivity of rates of return on a portfolio relative to general market movements. Beta represents systematic risk or risk that cannot be diversified away. Beta is the slope of a regression line and in finance is generally used to measure the percentage change in a single stock or of a portfolio relative to that of a benchmark index.

Correlation Coefficient:

A measure of the strength of the linear relationship between two sets of variables. A value of +1.00 indicates perfect positive correlation and -1.00 represents perfect negative correlation. A value of zero means the variables are not correlated. In portfolio applications, it can be used to measure the relationship between two stocks, a stock and an index, an index and a portfolio, or two asset classes, among others.

Information Ratio:

The Information Ratio is a measure of the risk adjusted excess return generated by a manager in relation to a benchmark. It is the ratio of that excess return to the tracking error. The standard deviation of the difference between portfolio return and benchmark return is an approximation of tracking error.

Sharpe Ratio:

A measure of a portfolio's excess return relative to the total variability of the portfolio. The Sharpe Ratio measures the efficiency of portfolio management - the excess return achieved per unit of risk taken. The ratio, developed by William Sharpe, is calculated as the ratio of the difference between the actual return and the risk free return to the standard deviation of the portfolio returns.

DEMARCHE FIXED INCOME UNIVERSE DEFINITIONS:

Short Duration:

Products with a benchmark of Barclays Government/Credit 1-3 or equivalent with all investment grade securities and no international.

Intermediate Government/Credit:

Products with a benchmark of Barclays Intermediate Government/Credit or equivalent with all investment grade securities and no international.

Government/Credit:

Products with a benchmark of Barclays Government/Credit or equivalent with all investment grade securities and no international.

Aggregate:

Products with a benchmark of Barclays Aggregate or equivalent with all investment grade securities and no international.

Core Plus:

Products with a benchmark of Barclays Government/Credit or Aggregate or equivalent with up to 15% in non-investment grade and/or non-dollar.

Core Extended:

Products with a benchmark of Barclays Government/Credit or Aggregate or equivalent with 15-50% in non-investment grade and/or non-dollar.

Long:

Products with a benchmark of Barclays Long Government/Credit or equivalent.

Rotational:

Products with an unrestricted duration range.

TIPS:

Products that invest exclusively in TIPS.

High Yield:

Products with more than 50% in non-investment grade issues.

REAL ESTATE INDEX DEFINITIONS:

NPI
(NCREIF Property Index)

An index of institutional grade real estate performance. It is intended to represent the total rate of return for unleveraged U.S. commercial real estate properties acquired in the private market for investment purposes only. The index tracks over 5,000 properties with an aggregate value over $175 Billion.

NFI-ODCE
(NCREIF Fund Index -
Open-End Diversified Core Equity)

An index of approximately 31 open-end core real estate funds. The index is a proxy for investment returns of private real estate held by institutional investors in the U.S. (DB plans, foundations and endowments). The maximum leverage that can be employed by the constituent funds in the index is 40%.
